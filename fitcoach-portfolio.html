Key insights
Booking friction is social, not logistical. Students hesitate to initiate a conversation. Self-service is the fix.
Memory is not the student's failure. The knowledge evaporates because there's nowhere for it to live after class.
Visual proof drives motivation. 4.5/5 on photo comparison — the highest rating across all questions.
The instructor's admin load is invisible but crushing. 30–60+ mins of WeChat daily, paper notebooks, pure memory.
02 — How · Design

The mediator that's always there

The coach can't be with students 24/7 — but the relationship needs to feel continuous. What if something could act as a proxy? Present during the 163 hours a week when the coach isn't.

That something is AI. Not as a replacement — but as connective tissue between sessions.

01
Remove the social cost of booking
A live calendar with self-booking means students never initiate a conversation just to secure a slot. No awkwardness, no delay.
02
Make the body a shared language
A visual body heatmap lets coaches annotate exactly where the issue is. Students see it, understand it, and the AI references it when generating plans.
03
Bring advice to the student at the right moment
AI daily reminders surface the coach's notes before a session, in the morning — not once at the end of class, then forgotten.
04
Let AI do the repetitive expert work
Training plan generation, recipe creation, progress summaries — AI handles the scaffold; the coach refines and personalises.
05
Make progress visible and motivating
Photos, comparisons, strength tracking — all in one place instead of scattered across WeChat albums and paper notebooks.

04 — Reflection

What I learned

The real user isn't always obvious
I started as a student. But the more I researched, the more I realised the instructor carried the heavier burden. Good research reframes who you're actually designing for.
Friction is often social, not technical
Students weren't failing to book because calendars were hard. They were avoiding the social act of initiating a chat. Solving the right problem required listening past the surface answer.
AI is best at the invisible work
The most compelling use of AI wasn't flashy — it was removing repetitive cognitive load: daily reminders, plan generation, compliance tracking. The stuff no one notices when it works.
Small sample, real signal
8 participants isn't statistically significant. But the qualitative patterns were remarkably consistent — 100% retention failure, 75% self-booking preference. These weren't flukes.
Next steps
Connect the prototype to a live Claude API — so AI reminders and plan generation are genuinely dynamic, not just illustrated.
Test with the actual instructor and one or two real students to validate whether the booking flow genuinely reduces friction.
Explore payment integration — several students flagged forgetting to pay, and the instructor expressed frustration about rescheduling disrupting revenue.
